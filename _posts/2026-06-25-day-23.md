what_i_learned: |
  Today I worked on the mid symposium presentation and focused on identifying the gaps, challenges, and expected outcomes of my research. I learned how important it is to clearly distinguish between general model performance and fairness-related performance in clinical AI systems. While deep learning models for ECG classification can achieve strong predictive accuracy, I realized that accuracy alone does not guarantee equitable outcomes across different patient groups. I also deepened my understanding of how historical clinical data can unintentionally encode demographic bias, which can then be learned and repeated by AI systems. This made me more aware that fairness has to be evaluated alongside accuracy rather than treated as an afterthought. Overall, I started to see my project not just as a technical modeling task, but as a clinical responsibility. In addition, I learned how to better structure my presentation so that complex ideas are communicated in a clear and logical flow. I spent time refining how I explain the instability of fairness metrics across multiple training runs, especially in ECG classifiers. It became clearer to me that even when a model architecture and dataset stay the same, randomness in initialization can still produce different fairness outcomes. This helped me connect reproducibility issues directly to clinical trust and patient safety concerns. I also began thinking more critically about intersectional subgroup analysis, especially age-by-sex groups, and why they matter in cardiovascular prediction tasks. By the end of the day, I felt more confident in framing my research as both a machine learning and healthcare equity problem.
  
blockers: |
  One of the main challenges I faced today was revising my introduction for the symposium presentation because it was initially too long and unfocused. I found myself trying to include too many technical details at once, which made the narrative harder to follow. I had to step back and decide which ideas were essential for explaining the problem and which ones could be simplified or moved to later sections. This process was difficult because I did not want to lose important scientific context, especially around ECG classification and fairness concerns. I also struggled with balancing technical accuracy with readability for a broader audience. Ultimately, I had to accept that clarity was more important than completeness in the introduction section. Another issue I encountered was making sure my writing aligned with academic expectations while still sounding natural and coherent. I kept revisiting sentences that felt too dense or overly technical, especially when discussing reproducibility and fairness instability. It was challenging to explain that models can vary across training runs without making the explanation sound repetitive or confusing. I also had to double-check that I was accurately representing how fairness metrics can fluctuate more than traditional performance metrics. This required me to re-read parts of my literature review to ensure consistency with prior studies. In the end, the revisions helped me improve the overall structure, but the process was more time-consuming than I expected.

reflection: |
  Reflecting on today’s work, I feel that my understanding of the project has become more grounded in real-world implications rather than just theoretical modeling. I am starting to appreciate how AI in healthcare is not only about building high-performing systems but also about ensuring those systems are reliable and fair across diverse populations. Thinking about ECG classification specifically made me realize how serious the consequences can be if a model misses a diagnosis for certain subgroups. This has made me more intentional about how I design my experiments and interpret my results. I also feel more connected to the broader goal of improving equity in clinical decision-making. Overall, today helped me see my research as something that can directly impact patient care and trust in AI systems. At the same time, I feel excited about the direction my project is taking, especially as I begin to focus on fairness stability across multiple runs. The idea that fairness itself can change depending on randomness in training is both challenging and interesting to explore. It pushes me to think beyond single-number evaluations and toward more robust, reproducible research methods. I also feel more motivated to continue refining my presentation because I can see how important it is for communicating these ideas clearly. Even though the revision process was frustrating at times, it helped me strengthen both my writing and my understanding of the research. Moving forward, I want to keep improving how I connect technical results to meaningful clinical insights.
