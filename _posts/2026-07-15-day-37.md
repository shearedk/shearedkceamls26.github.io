what_i_learned: |
  Today was focused on preparing for one of the biggest milestones of our summer research experience. Throughout the morning, my team finalized our elevator pitch to ensure that it stayed within the required 90-second time limit while still communicating the purpose, significance, and findings of our research. Because every second mattered, we carefully evaluated each sentence to determine whether it contributed to the overall message or could be shortened without losing meaning. This process taught me that effective science communication is not about saying everything you know but about identifying the most important information that your audience needs to understand. Our research investigates predictive stability versus fairness instability in deep learning models used for clinical ECG classification, and condensing such a technical topic into a short presentation required thoughtful planning and multiple revisions. By the end of our practice sessions, I felt much more confident that our presentation clearly explains why fairness should be evaluated alongside accuracy when developing artificial intelligence systems for healthcare. In addition to refining our elevator pitch, we spent a significant portion of the day working on the methodology section of our research paper. We drafted the methodology in Overleaf and carefully described each stage of our experimental design so that another researcher could reproduce our work. We also searched for additional peer-reviewed journal articles to support our methodological decisions and strengthen the scientific foundation of our paper. Reading these publications helped me better understand how experienced researchers justify their methods and explain why specific techniques are appropriate for answering a research question. I realized that writing the methodology is much more than documenting the steps we completed; it requires explaining the reasoning behind every decision, from dataset selection to model evaluation and fairness analysis. This experience helped me appreciate the importance of transparency and reproducibility in scientific research, especially when working with artificial intelligence in healthcare.

blockers: |
  Fortunately, today did not present any major obstacles that prevented us from making progress, but there were still challenges that required careful attention. One of the biggest challenges was making sure our elevator pitch remained under the 90-second limit while still including all of the essential components of our research. Every revision required us to think critically about which details were necessary and which could be simplified without changing the meaning of our message. Although it was difficult to remove information that we felt was important, I learned that concise communication is an important skill for researchers because audiences often have limited time and attention. We practiced multiple times to improve our timing, pacing, and transitions so that the presentation would feel natural rather than rushed. These revisions ultimately strengthened our presentation by making it clearer and more engaging. Another challenge involved developing the methodology section of our manuscript. Although we understood the procedures we followed throughout the project, translating those technical processes into formal scientific writing required careful wording and organization. We wanted to ensure that every methodological step was described accurately while also being supported by relevant scholarly literature. Finding articles that aligned with our research design took time, but doing so strengthened the credibility of our paper and helped us justify our experimental approach. Rather than viewing this as a setback, I saw it as an opportunity to deepen my understanding of research methodology and scientific writing. By the end of the day, I felt that our methodology section had become much stronger and was moving closer to publication-quality work.
