what_i_learned: |
  Today our research team continued training our deep learning models and evaluating their performance across different demographic groups. The primary goal of today's work was to compare how well each model classified ECG data while also measuring whether certain patient groups experienced higher rates of missed diagnoses. Instead of focusing only on overall predictive accuracy, we examined the False Negative Rate (FNR), which measures how often the model incorrectly predicts that a patient does not have a condition when they actually do. In a healthcare setting, false negatives are especially important because they represent missed diagnoses that could delay treatment and negatively affect patient outcomes. Analyzing these subgroup-level metrics helped me better understand why fairness evaluation is essential when developing clinical AI systems. Today's experiments reinforced that a model can appear highly accurate overall while still performing differently across specific demographic groups. We also discussed the next phase of our project, which involves developing a new model that reduces bias by improving performance for groups that are currently underperforming. One approach we plan to investigate is sampling techniques that increase the representation of groups with higher error rates during model training. We also discussed other bias mitigation strategies that encourage the model to give greater attention to demographic groups experiencing higher False Negative Rates. Today's results showed meaningful improvements when comparing the original 1D CNN with the CNN Transformer model. For example, the False Negative Rate for older males decreased from approximately 36.4% to 13.3%, while the rate for older females decreased from approximately 33.7% to 12.8%. Similar improvements were observed for younger males and younger females, suggesting that the CNN Transformer not only improved predictive performance but also reduced disparities across the demographic groups we evaluated.

blockers: |
  Although today's experiments produced encouraging results, one challenge was interpreting what the fairness metrics actually meant in the context of healthcare. Looking only at the numbers does not immediately explain why one subgroup performs better than another or what factors contribute to those differences. We spent time discussing how differences in sample size, data representation, and model architecture can all influence subgroup performance. Understanding these factors is important because our goal is not simply to build a more accurate model but to develop one that performs consistently across different patient populations. This required careful interpretation of the results rather than assuming that lower error rates automatically indicate complete fairness. Today's discussions reminded me that evaluating fairness requires both technical analysis and thoughtful scientific reasoning. Another challenge involves the work that still lies ahead as we begin implementing bias mitigation techniques. While the CNN Transformer clearly reduced the False Negative Rate for every demographic group compared to the original 1D CNN, there is still room for improvement before we can conclude that the model is equitable. Our next step will be testing methods such as resampling and other fairness-aware training strategies to determine whether we can further reduce disparities without sacrificing predictive performance. These experiments will require additional model training, careful evaluation, and repeated comparisons across demographic groups. Although this process will take time, I understand that developing trustworthy artificial intelligence requires continuous testing and refinement. I am excited to see whether these mitigation techniques can further improve both fairness and model reliability.
