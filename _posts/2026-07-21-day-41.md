what_i_learned: |
  Today was dedicated to preparing our final research presentation for the SAIRI symposium. My primary responsibility was developing the sections titled Challenges & Adaptations, Limitations of Prior Research, and Research Gap. Working on these sections required me to revisit our literature review and think critically about what previous studies had accomplished and, more importantly, what questions still remained unanswered. I learned that identifying a research gap is much more than stating that something has not been studied before. Instead, it requires explaining why that missing knowledge is important, how it affects the field, and how our project contributes new evidence that advances current understanding. Through this process, I gained a greater appreciation for how every research study builds upon previous work while creating opportunities for future investigation. I also learned that creating an effective presentation requires a different mindset than writing a research paper. A research paper contains detailed explanations and extensive technical information, while a presentation must communicate the same ideas clearly, accurately, and within a limited amount of time. As I worked on my slides and speaker notes, I focused on making sure that every point aligned with the information presented in our manuscript while remaining easy for the audience to follow. I spent time reviewing the literature review and introduction to ensure that my presentation accurately reflected our research question and findings. This process reinforced the importance of consistency because the presentation should tell the same story as the paper without overwhelming the audience with unnecessary details. By the end of the day, I felt more confident in my understanding of our project's purpose and the contributions it makes to fairness research in clinical artificial intelligence.

blockers: |
  One challenge I experienced today was writing a presentation script that sounded natural while still maintaining scientific accuracy. It was tempting to read directly from the research paper because the manuscript contains detailed explanations, but I quickly realized that language appropriate for a journal article does not always translate well into an oral presentation. I had to rewrite several sections using simpler language without changing the meaning of our research. Finding the right balance between technical depth and audience engagement required multiple revisions and careful practice. Another difficulty was deciding how much information to include on each slide because I wanted the slides to support my presentation instead of becoming paragraphs of text. Working through these revisions helped me better understand how effective presentations depend on clarity, organization, and confident delivery rather than simply presenting as much information as possible. Another challenge involved ensuring that my presentation matched the overall flow of our research paper. Since I was responsible for discussing the limitations of prior research and our project's research gap, I needed to make sure my slides connected naturally to the sections presented by my teammates. This required reviewing the entire presentation several times so that my transitions would feel smooth and logical. I also found it challenging to explain why previous studies were limited without minimizing the important contributions they made to the field. Instead, I learned to emphasize that our work builds upon existing research by investigating predictive stability and fairness instability across repeated training runs using intersectional subgroup analysis. Although preparing these sections required careful thought and several revisions, I believe the final result more accurately represents the significance of our research.

reflection: |
  Reflecting on today, I realized how much my confidence has grown since the beginning of the SAIRI program. Earlier this summer, I would have felt intimidated by presenting topics such as deep learning, fairness metrics, reproducibility, and ECG classification to an audience. Now I feel much more comfortable discussing these concepts because I have spent weeks reading scientific literature, writing our manuscript, analyzing results, and collaborating with my research team. While I am still nervous about speaking in front of others, I recognize that my understanding of the project has improved tremendously. I know that confidence comes from preparation, and today's work reminded me that practicing my presentation is just as important as creating the slides themselves. Every opportunity to present helps me become a stronger communicator and prepares me for future academic and professional presentations. As I look ahead to our final symposium, I feel both excited and grateful for everything I have learned throughout this internship. This experience has challenged me to think critically, communicate more effectively, and develop skills that extend far beyond programming or artificial intelligence. I have learned how to read scientific literature more efficiently, write sections of a research paper, collaborate within a multidisciplinary team, and explain complex technical concepts to broader audiences. These are skills that I know will benefit me throughout graduate school and my future career in healthcare and research. Although I still have areas where I want to improve, particularly my public speaking, I am proud of the progress I have made over the past several weeks. Overall, today's work reminded me that successful research is not only about discovering new knowledge but also about communicating that knowledge clearly, accurately, and with confidence.
