what_i_learned: |
  Today our research team focused on comparing the performance of our baseline model with the newer deep learning architectures that we have been developing throughout the project. We analyzed the results from each model to determine not only which one produced the highest predictive performance but also which model demonstrated the greatest fairness across different age-by-sex demographic groups. In addition, we continued training our hybrid CNN model to determine whether combining convolutional neural networks with transformer-based components could further improve both predictive accuracy and fairness. Watching the models continue to evolve helped me better understand that machine learning research is an iterative process that requires repeated testing, evaluation, and refinement. Every experiment provides additional evidence that helps guide our next decisions and improve the overall quality of the project. Today's work reinforced the importance of comparing multiple approaches before determining which model is most appropriate for clinical applications. We also spent a significant amount of time brainstorming ideas for our final symposium elevator pitch. Since we only have 90 seconds to introduce our research, we discussed how to communicate our project in a way that is both engaging and understandable to individuals who may not have a background in artificial intelligence or healthcare. I learned that an effective elevator pitch is not simply a shortened presentation but a carefully designed message that quickly explains the problem, why it matters, and how our research addresses it. We practiced simplifying technical language without losing the scientific significance of our work. This exercise challenged me to think about my research from the audience's perspective rather than my own. By the end of the discussion, I realized that strong communication skills are just as important as strong technical skills when presenting research.

blockers: |
  Although our experiments are progressing well, one challenge we continue to face is the amount of time required to train each machine learning model before we can compare the results. Every time we modify the architecture or adjust model parameters, we must rerun the experiments and carefully analyze the outcomes before deciding on the next step. This process requires patience because meaningful improvements cannot be measured immediately. We also have to compare several evaluation metrics rather than relying on overall accuracy alone. Understanding how each model performs across different demographic groups requires careful analysis and thoughtful interpretation. Despite these challenges, every experiment moves us one step closer to identifying the most reliable and equitable model for ECG classification. Another challenge involved developing an elevator pitch that effectively summarizes several weeks of research within only 90 seconds. Our project includes concepts such as deep learning, ECG classification, fairness evaluation, reproducibility, and bias mitigation, making it difficult to determine which details are the most important to include. We wanted our message to be informative without overwhelming the audience with technical terminology. Brainstorming different approaches helped us recognize that the most effective presentations focus on the real-world problem before explaining the technical solution. This discussion encouraged us to think creatively about how to make our research memorable and accessible. I believe these conversations will help us deliver a stronger presentation during the final symposium.

One idea we have for our pitch is imagine two patients visit the hospital with similar heart conditions. One receives an accurate diagnosis, while the other is overlooked simply because the AI model performs differently for their demographic group. Our research investigates whether deep learning models used to classify ECGs are equally reliable for everyone. We compare different deep learning architectures and evaluate fairness across age-by-sex groups using False Negative Rate, which measures missed diagnoses. We then explore bias mitigation techniques to reduce these disparities. Our goal is to build AI systems that are not only accurate, but also fair and trustworthy for every patient.
