what_i_learned: |
  Today was a fairly straightforward but still meaningful day in the research lab. Most of my time was spent working within our weekly lab environment and preparing for our group presentation. In the showcase, I explained my work on a machine learning project focused on training a 1D Convolutional Neural Network, also called a 1D-CNN. This type of model is designed to learn patterns from sequential data, such as ECG heart signals that change over time. Presenting this helped me better understand how to explain technical work in a way that is clear and structured. It also pushed me to connect what I am doing in code to real-world medical applications. Another key part of what I learned today came from building my confidence with tools and technical concepts. I worked with Overleaf, which is an online platform used for writing professional research papers in a clean and organized format. At first, it felt unfamiliar and slightly overwhelming, but I started to understand how useful it is for academic writing and collaboration. I also successfully trained a 1D-CNN model using the PTB-XL ECG dataset, which contains real heart signal recordings. We ran the model using three different seeds, which are different starting points that affect randomness, and trained it for sixty epochs, meaning the model learned by passing through the dataset sixty times. This helped me see how repetition and structure improve model performance over time.

blockers: |
  There were no major blockers that stopped me from completing my tasks today, but I did experience a few small challenges that slowed me down. One of the main things I noticed was that I still need more time to fully understand how all parts of the training loop connect together. The training loop is the full process where the model makes predictions, checks its errors, and updates itself to improve. Even though I followed along, it sometimes felt like I was completing steps without fully seeing the full picture at once. I had to pause a few times and mentally break down what each section of the code was doing. That helped me avoid confusion, but it also showed me that I am still building foundational understanding. Another small challenge was dealing with technical language and debugging. Some of the terminology like convolution, which means sliding filters across data to detect patterns, and tensors, which are structured numerical arrays used in machine learning, still takes time for me to fully internalize. When errors appeared in the code, it was not always obvious where the problem was coming from, so I had to carefully check each step. Even though it was frustrating at moments, it did not stop me from completing the lab. Instead, it forced me to slow down and think more critically about what I was doing. Overall, these were manageable challenges rather than true blockers, and they helped me learn in the process.

Overall, I am looking forward to this next stage and seeing how much further I can grow from here.
