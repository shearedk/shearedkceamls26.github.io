what_i_learned: |
  Today, our team focused on writing the limitations section of our research paper, which helped me better understand the importance of being honest and transparent about what a study can and cannot prove. One major limitation is that the PTB-XL dataset was collected from a single German clinical population and does not provide information about race or ethnicity. Because of this, our intersectional fairness analysis is limited to age and sex, even though previous research has shown that race-based disparities may also affect the performance of ECG deep learning systems. I learned that this limitation does not only apply to our research design, but also reflects a broader weakness within the available dataset. Without complete demographic information, researchers may be unable to identify important disparities affecting underrepresented patient populations. Writing about this issue reminded me that the quality and diversity of a dataset strongly influence the types of fairness questions researchers are able to investigate. I also learned more about the limitations of our statistical validation, robustness testing, fairness mitigation, and explainability methods. Our study used three random seeds, which allowed us to observe meaningful changes in subgroup False Negative Rate, but three training runs are not enough to produce narrow or highly precise confidence intervals. We also varied only the random initialization and data-shuffling order while keeping the architecture, hyperparameters, and data split the same, meaning that the instability we observed may represent only a portion of the model’s total variability. In addition, our robustness experiment used a simplified low-frequency sinusoidal signal to represent baseline wander, even though real ECG recordings may contain muscle noise, powerline interference, electrode movement, and incorrectly placed leads. Our fairness mitigation analysis tested only inverse-frequency demographic reweighting and used a single classification threshold of 0.5, so other fairness-aware methods may produce different outcomes. Finally, our Grad-CAM analysis was qualitative and performed on a limited number of examples, which means the visualizations should be treated as exploratory evidence rather than proof of exactly how the model made its decisions.

blockers: |
  One blocker today was deciding how to write the limitations without making our research appear weak or unsuccessful. At first, it felt uncomfortable to describe areas where our dataset, experimental design, or evaluation methods could have been more comprehensive. However, I began to understand that acknowledging limitations is a sign of strong research because it allows readers to interpret the results more accurately. We had to carefully explain that our findings remain meaningful while also recognizing that they should not be generalized beyond the populations, models, and experimental conditions included in the study. It was especially challenging to describe the wide confidence interval for the older-female myocardial infarction False Negative Rate without minimizing the importance of the observed instability. Working through this challenge taught me that scientific writing requires both confidence in the findings and humility about the conclusions that can reasonably be made. Another challenge was beginning to divide the slides for our final research presentation among the members of our group. We needed to make sure that each person received a fair amount of content while also assigning sections that matched their understanding and speaking strengths. I am excited to present our research, but I am also nervous because public speaking is still an area that I want to improve. I sometimes worry about forgetting important information, speaking too quickly, or depending too heavily on the words written on the slides. To address this, I plan to practice my section multiple times, focus on understanding the meaning behind each point, and become comfortable explaining the research without memorizing every sentence exactly. Although the final presentation creates some pressure, I believe it will give me an important opportunity to develop greater confidence and become a more effective scientific communicator

reflection: |
  Reflecting on today, I appreciate that writing the limitations section forced me to examine our study from a more critical and realistic perspective. Throughout the program, much of our attention has focused on the strengths of our research, including the use of multiple models, intersectional fairness analysis, repeated training runs, robustness testing, and explainability techniques. Today reminded me that no research project is perfect, regardless of how carefully it is designed. Every dataset has boundaries, every model is influenced by design decisions, and every evaluation method provides only one perspective on the system being studied. Recognizing these limitations does not erase the value of our findings; instead, it helps clarify what future researchers should investigate next. I feel that this process has helped me think more like a responsible researcher who values accuracy, transparency, and ethical interpretation. I am also reflecting on how much I want to grow through the final presentation experience. I feel both excited and nervous because this presentation represents the conclusion of weeks of coding, reading, writing, debugging, and collaborating with my research team. I want the audience to understand that our project, “Predictive Stability versus Fairness Instability in Clinical ECG Classification,” examines whether consistently accurate AI models are also consistently fair across age-by-sex patient groups. I know that communicating this message effectively will require me to speak clearly, maintain eye contact, control my pacing, and explain technical concepts in a way that is understandable. Instead of allowing my nervousness to discourage me, I want to use it as motivation to prepare more carefully and strengthen my public speaking skills. Overall, I am proud of the progress we have made, and I am looking forward to presenting our work with confidence while continuing to learn from the experience.
