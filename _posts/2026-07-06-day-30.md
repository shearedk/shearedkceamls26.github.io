what_i_learned: |
  Today we continued improving our deep learning model by running our code and evaluating whether it produced disparities across different demographic groups. Our goal was not only to improve the model's predictive performance but also to determine whether the model behaved fairly across age-by-sex subgroups. Because our experiments require repeated model training, each run takes a considerable amount of time to complete before we can evaluate the results. While waiting for the model to finish training, I spent time reviewing the outputs and discussing possible improvements with my research team. This process helped me better understand how changes in model training can influence both predictive accuracy and fairness metrics. I also realized that developing trustworthy artificial intelligence requires careful evaluation of both technical performance and equitable outcomes. In addition to working on the code, I read the research article "An Artificial Intelligence-Enabled ECG Algorithm for the Identification of Patients with Atrial Fibrillation During Sinus Rhythm" by Attia et al. (2019). The study introduced a convolutional neural network that analyzed standard 10-second, 12-lead ECGs collected during normal sinus rhythm to identify patients with atrial fibrillation, even when the abnormal rhythm was not present at the time of the ECG. Using more than 180,000 patients and nearly 650,000 ECG recordings from the Mayo Clinic, the researchers trained and tested a deep learning model that achieved an area under the curve (AUC) of approximately 0.87 using a single ECG and improved to 0.90 when multiple ECGs from the same patient were analyzed. I found it fascinating that the model could detect subtle electrical patterns that are invisible to the human eye, demonstrating the power of deep learning in cardiovascular medicine. Reading this paper helped me understand why large datasets, convolutional neural networks, and rigorous model evaluation are essential for developing clinically useful AI systems. It also reinforced the importance of my own research because, while this study focused on predictive accuracy, my project investigates whether those accurate predictions remain fair and consistent across different demographic groups.

blockers: |
  The primary challenge I encountered today was the amount of time required to train and evaluate our deep learning model. Because we are running multiple experiments to compare predictive performance and fairness across repeated training runs, each experiment requires significant computational time before results become available. This means that progress can sometimes feel slow, especially when we must wait for one experiment to finish before making additional adjustments. Rather than viewing this as wasted time, I used the waiting period to review research articles, organize notes, and prepare for the next stage of the project. This helped me remain productive while also strengthening my understanding of the scientific literature. I learned that patience is an important part of machine learning research because careful experimentation cannot be rushed. Another challenge involved interpreting the results after each model completed training. Improving model accuracy alone is not enough because we also need to examine whether the model performs consistently across different patient populations. This requires comparing multiple evaluation metrics, including fairness measures such as the False Negative Rate for different demographic groups. Understanding these results takes careful analysis because improvements in one metric do not always translate to improvements in another. Working through these challenges has helped me become more comfortable interpreting machine learning experiments and thinking critically about model performance. Every experiment provides additional insight into how fairness and reproducibility interact within clinical AI systems.
