what_i_learned: |
  Today I spent most of my time conducting a literature review using Google Scholar to identify research articles related to fairness and reproducibility in machine learning. One of the most interesting articles I read was "Are My Deep Learning Systems Fair? An Empirical Study of Fixed-Seed Training." The purpose of the study was to investigate whether deep learning models can produce different fairness outcomes even when researchers use the same dataset, model architecture, hyperparameters, and random seed. Before reading this article, I assumed that fixing a random seed would make model results fully reproducible. However, the authors demonstrated that software implementation factors such as GPU operations, floating-point computations, and software parallelism can still create substantial differences in fairness outcomes. This finding helped me understand that fairness in machine learning is much more complex than simply achieving high predictive accuracy. Another important lesson I learned was that fairness should be treated as a distribution rather than a single measurement. The researchers found that fairness metrics such as Demographic Parity and Disparate Impact could vary significantly across identical training runs. In some cases, one model appeared completely fair while another identical run appeared completely unfair for the same demographic subgroup. The article also showed that many debiasing techniques fail to consistently improve fairness when evaluated across multiple training runs. I learned that researchers should report fairness metrics with measures of variability, including standard deviations, confidence intervals, and statistical significance tests. These findings directly relate to our ECG fairness project because they support the idea that predictive performance may remain stable while fairness metrics fluctuate substantially across runs.

The statistical methods discussed in the paper, including confidence intervals and significance testing, also required careful attention because they play an important role in evaluating fairness variability. Although these concepts were challenging at first, they became clearer as I connected them to our ECG classification research. This process reinforced the importance of continuously reviewing and engaging with complex research literature.
