what_i_learned: |
  Today I learned about AI fairness and why it is such an important topic in healthcare. Before this lecture, I mainly thought about whether an AI model was accurate, but I did not realize that a model could be accurate overall while still treating certain groups unfairly. We discussed how artificial intelligence can unintentionally amplify healthcare disparities if some groups are underrepresented in the training data. I learned about several fairness metrics, including False Negative Rate (FNR), False Positive Rate (FPR), Equal Opportunity, Equalized Odds, and Demographic Parity. Since I do not have a computer science background, many of these terms were completely new to me. However, the examples related to healthcare helped me understand why fairness matters and how biased predictions can negatively affect patient outcomes. I also spent time becoming more familiar with Overleaf and searching for scholarly articles related to my research project, Predictive Stability vs. Fairness Instability in Clinical ECG Classification: A Multi-Run Analysis of Deep Learning Models. Reading research papers helped me better understand how fairness and predictive performance are evaluated in machine learning studies. I learned that researchers often use datasets such as PTB-XL to train models that can classify ECG recordings and identify heart conditions. Another interesting concept I learned was that fairness metrics can sometimes vary across multiple training runs even when overall model accuracy remains stable. This idea is central to our project because it challenges the assumption that fairness is always consistent. Overall, I gained a better understanding of both the healthcare implications and technical foundations of our research.

blockers: |
  One challenge I faced today was understanding the large number of new technical terms introduced during the lecture. As a Psychological Sciences major, I am much more familiar with research methods and healthcare concepts than I am with machine learning terminology. Concepts such as Equalized Odds, Demographic Parity, and False Negative Rate required me to spend extra time reviewing notes and asking questions. While the instructors explained these concepts clearly, I still needed additional examples before I fully understood how they related to model fairness. I found myself comparing these ideas to concepts from psychology research in order to make them easier to understand. Although this process took time, it helped me gradually build confidence in my understanding. Another challenge was reading scholarly articles that contained advanced computer science and statistical language. Many of the papers assumed prior knowledge of machine learning concepts that I am still learning. At times, it was difficult to follow the methodology sections because of unfamiliar terminology and mathematical notation. I also had to spend extra time learning how to navigate and use Overleaf effectively since it was my first time working with the platform. Despite these challenges, I was able to identify several articles relevant to our project and begin understanding the broader research landscape. I know that becoming comfortable with these tools and concepts will require patience and consistent practice throughout the program.
