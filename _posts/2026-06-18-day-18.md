what_i_learned: |
  Today was another interesting learning experience because I was introduced to several concepts that were completely new to me. During Dr. Shaun Gittens' lecture, I learned about tokens and their role in artificial intelligence systems. Before today, I thought of tokens as simply words or pieces of text, but I learned that they actually represent a model's work capacity and reasoning resources. One idea that stood out to me was that unused tokens can mean unused opportunities for reasoning, memory, and exploration within an AI system. We also discussed how the role of people working with AI is shifting from simply coding to planning, evaluating, and orchestrating how these systems are used. Since I do not come from a computer science background, some of these concepts were challenging at first, but the lecture helped me better understand how modern AI systems function behind the scenes. I also learned about different types of AI architectures beyond traditional transformers. Before this lecture, I had only heard about transformers because they are commonly used in many popular AI systems today. Dr. Gittens explained that future AI systems may combine multiple architectures, including state-space models and recurrent or liquid networks, to improve efficiency and performance. I found it interesting that researchers are not necessarily trying to replace transformers but instead are looking for ways to specialize and combine different approaches. In addition to the lecture, I spent time reviewing a research article on algorithmic fairness in healthcare AI. The article helped me understand that healthcare disparities often exist before AI is introduced and that AI models can unintentionally learn and amplify those disparities. This literature review strengthened my understanding of why fairness is such an important component of our ECG classification research project.
  
blockers: |
  No major blockers prevented me from making progress today, but I did encounter several concepts that required additional effort to   understand. Today was interpreting some of the technical details presented in the research article. The article discussed multiple fairness metrics, fairness interventions, dataset shift, and accuracy-fairness tradeoffs, which required careful reading to fully understand. I noticed that many of the concepts connected directly to our research project, but it took time to understand how those connections were being made. Some of the statistical and machine learning terminology was difficult to follow because I have limited experience with those topics. Despite this challenge, I was able to identify several key findings that support our project's focus on fairness variability across multiple model runs. Although the material was complex, I felt more confident by the end of the day than when I first started reading. Terms such as transformers, state-space models, and liquid networks were new concepts that I had not previously studied. I found myself needing to pause and look up definitions in order to better understand how these systems differ from one another. While the lecture was informative, it sometimes felt like I was learning an entirely new language. Fortunately, taking detailed notes and asking questions helped me overcome some of this initial confusion.

reflection: |
  Reflecting on today, I am realizing how much I am learning outside of my normal academic comfort zone. This program has exposed me to artificial intelligence, machine learning, and computational research in ways that I never expected. While there are still many technical concepts that I do not fully understand, I am beginning to see how they connect to important healthcare issues. Learning about fairness in AI has been especially meaningful because it combines technology with concerns about equity, ethics, and patient outcomes. These are topics that align closely with my long-term goal of working in healthcare and helping underserved populations. I was particularly interested in learning that fairness metrics can behave differently from traditional performance metrics. The literature review showed that a model can appear accurate while still producing unfair outcomes for certain demographic groups. This idea directly relates to our project's hypothesis that fairness metrics such as False Negative Rate may vary across runs even when AUROC remains stable. Understanding this concept has helped me better appreciate the importance of evaluating AI systems from multiple perspectives rather than focusing only on accuracy. Although I still have a lot to learn, I am becoming more comfortable reading research papers and discussing technical concepts. Overall, today reinforced the importance of fairness, stability, and reliability in healthcare AI and reminded me why this research has real-world significance.
